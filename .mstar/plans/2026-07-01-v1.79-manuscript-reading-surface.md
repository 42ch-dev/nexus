## Goal

Promote the post-V1.75-pivot residual `chapter-page.tsx` (currently a bare read-only body render + frontmatter strip + Copy Path + "Edit outline → Canvas" redirect) into a **designed manuscript reading surface** with legible reading typography, chapter/volume navigation, reading progress, and **in-context lightweight maturation indicators** (chapter completion-state badge, World KB density count, open-findings count). Read-only consumption of existing data. **No new write routes** — body-ownership invariant preserved (canvas remains the sole authoring surface per V1.75 pivot).

Architectural verdict (P-1): no new count endpoint is needed for the V1.79 MVP. Existing read-only surfaces suffice:

- Open-findings count: use `GET /v1/local/works/{work_id}/findings` via `list_findings_handler` with comma-separated `status` filters (`open,triaged,in_review`) and optional `chapter`; paginate if exact counts exceed one page. Do not add a write or mutation route.
- World KB density count: use `GET /v1/local/worlds/{world_id}/kb/graph` via `world_kb::get_graph` and count non-deleted `entities` in the returned graph projection. This is lightweight and read-only for V1.79; a future dedicated aggregate endpoint is only justified if graph payload size becomes a measured bottleneck.

### D. User stories

The reading surface serves one core author need — reviewing prose in a designed reading experience — with maturation indicators that make the review actionable. Every story below is verifiable against the shipped surface without backend or CLI intervention.

- **Read a chapter as a reader** — *As an author*, I can open any chapter and read it in a comfortable, distraction-free reading view with legible typography (light and dark), so I can review my prose the way a reader would experience it — not as raw markdown with YAML frontmatter noise. The typography feels book-like; I am not squinting at monospace or fighting line lengths.
- **Navigate my manuscript** — *As an author*, I can move between chapters (prev/next, keyboard ←/→) and jump across volumes, so I can flow through my manuscript naturally while reading — not open and close individual files one at a time.
- **Pick up where I left off** — *As an author* reading mid-session, my scroll position is preserved within the session, so I can navigate away and come back without losing my place.
- **See chapter maturity at a glance** — *As an author*, I can look at a chapter's completion-state badge, World KB density count, and open-findings count without navigating away or opening a dashboard, so I immediately know: "this chapter is finalized, has rich world-building, but still has 3 open quality issues." The indicators are interpretable without tooltips or documentation.
- **Stay oriented during review** — *As an author* moving through chapters while addressing findings, the open-findings count changes as I resolve issues (via the findings page), so the reading surface reflects the current quality state of each chapter without a manual refresh.

These stories define product success for Track A. The maturation indicators are the primary differentiating value — they make the reading surface a **review-augmented reading surface**, not just a markdown renderer with margins.

### E. Profile scope — product justification

Novel-first scope is a product decision, not a technical limitation. Body render is profile-agnostic, so essays, game-bibles, and scripts all benefit from the reading surface on day one (typography, navigation, progress). Profile-specific chrome (e.g., essay section-break styling, game-bible entity cross-reference overlays) would be speculative without usage data from novel-first launch. The risk of building profile-specific features for a surface without proven engagement is higher than the risk of non-novel profiles being "underserved" — the body render alone is a net improvement over the pre-V1.79 bare chapter body view for all profiles.
